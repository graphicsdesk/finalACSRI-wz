Over the past three decades, several companies have been frequent divestment targets for pro-Palestinian activists, including Boeing, Caterpillar, Lockheed Martin, and Raytheon, a division of RTX.

The 2002 faculty petition, rejected by the ACSRI, cites Boeing and General Electric as makers of Apache helicopters, which the authors write are “regularly used by the Israeli military in carrying out extra-judicial killings.” It also cites Caterpillar as the maker of D9 bulldozers which have been used to “to raze entire neighborhoods” in Palestinian territories.

The Eye obtained a draft version of a 2007 Iraq War divestment proposal. Proposal writer David Judd, SEAS ’08, called the ACSRI’s rejection of the proposal unsurprising. “We hadn’t built the momentum that we would have needed to in order to get it to happen,” Judd said in an April 2025 interview with The Eye.

ACSRI continued considering proposals that demanded divestment from arms in 2008 and then an updated proposal to divest from cluster munitions in 2009. ACSRI responded with subcommittees to provide more research on arms and cluster munitions.

The ACSRI ultimately never made a recommendation to the board of trustees.

On April 28, 2016, CUAD submitted a proposal to the ACSRI calling for divestment from companies that “profit from the State of Israel’s violations of international law,” citing Columbia’s direct investment in HD Hyundai Infracore, then known as Doosan Infracore, because of the company’s ties to an “illegal settlement in the West Bank.” The CUAD proposal, supported by a faculty-led petition, targeted eight additional corporations without definitive knowledge of whether Columbia had holdings in them due to the opacity of Columbia’s investment portfolio.

The ACSRI said it “received” the proposal but could not review it that academic year. In meeting minutes and the annual report from the next academic year, the ACSRI did not mention the proposal. The Eye reported in 2019 that CUAD no longer engaged with the ACSRI, having lost faith in its effectiveness.

CUAD, in its new configuration, wrote a proposal to the ACSRI targeting Columbia’s direct investments—like Airbnb—or corporations that were sole benefactors of trusts that provide Columbia grants and contributions.

CUAD highlights that Airbnb had listed 39 settlements in the occupied West Bank at the time of its proposal and that Microsoft provides services to the Israeli Ministry of Defense.

In 2024, activists submitted three proposals related to divestment from Israel and worldwide human rights violations that the ACSRI has yet to respond to. All three list AMETEK and Teledyne Technologies, reflecting their perceived significance as publicly traded securities that the Columbia Investment Management Company manages as direct holdings.

Additional companies are referenced in a shareholder proposal, which asks the University to demand companies in its investment portfolio cease activities that contribute to “human rights violations and violations of U.S. export and foreign assistance laws which infringe upon the rights of Palestinians in Israel, Occupied Palestinian Territories, and the Palestinian Diaspora.”

Teledyne is an American defense contractor that has at least 124 contracts supplying weapons to Israel, according to the proposals. Campaign Against Arms Trade, a UK-based organization working to end the international arms trade, lists the most recent contract between Teledyne and Israel from May 2024.

AMETEK produces cooling components for F-16 and F-53 fighter jets. Two of the proposals cite a 2009 airstrike where the United Nations documented the Israeli Air Force’s use of F-16 jets to conduct airstrikes in Gaza during Operation Cast Lead. During that 22-day assault, Israel’s military killed at least 1,400 Palestinians, 300 of whom were children, according to the proposals.

Prior divestment proposals were able to cite companies on Columbia’s list of direct holdings alongside the specific amount of investment. But the list featuring AMETEK and Teledyne is devoid of any dollar amounts.

A Columbia spokesperson declined to comment when asked by The Eye why and when this change occurred.
